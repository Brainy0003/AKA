Debugging

If your first coding attempt compiled successfully on the first try, you are one lucky bastard!
Generally your code isn't just going to compile right the first time, unless you are the most unbelievably accurate and organized person around .
Most of the time, you will have to debug your code a bit.
If your first code example did compile succesfully, let's mess it up a bit. Remove one of the semicolons and compile again. You will get a list of one or more errors.
When you do, it will give you an error number, followed by the line number where the error occurred, and a brief description of the type of error. When this happens, look at the number. Read it out loud. Read what the error message actually says, then check the line number and go back and see if you can spot your error. While those numbers don't mean much to you now, before long you will begin to recognize various error messages. Soon you will look and see Error 1702 and intuitively know that you forgot to add a semicolon or whatever. Some errors can cause a whole list of errors down the line and you may think it's completely messed up, when all along maybe you had a colon instead of a semicolon, or a period instead of a comma.

Note: that just because the compiler says the problem is on one line, it may be on the line before, or somewhere in the vicinity. This is just where the compiler ran into a glitch. But hopefully that will get you in the general area.

Take a moment to try a few different things to your code and observe the error messages. Try leaving out opening or closing brackets. Try a colon instead of a semicolon, etc..

Think of bugs in your code as a challenge to your intellect. A puzzle to be solved.

Note: in Notepad you can tell what line number you are on by going to View / Status bar. Insert your cursor and scroll up and down using the arrow keys. The line number appears in the bar at the bottom.
